Understanding Alzheimer's

Alzheimer's disease is a progressive neurological disorder that causes the brain to shrink (atrophy) and brain cells to die. It is the most common cause of dementia.

What Happens?

The disease is characterized by two key abnormalities: **Amyloid Plaques** (deposits of protein fragments that build up in the spaces between nerve cells) and **Tau Tangles** (twisted fibers of Tau protein that build up inside the nerve cells).

Stages of Progression

Alzheimer's progresses through three main stages: **Mild** (MCI/Early-Stage, where the person maintains independence), **Moderate** (Middle-Stage, requiring significant assistance), and **Severe** (Late-Stage, requiring full-time care).

Caring Resources

Research suggests that a combination of lifestyle factors can help reduce the risk or slow the progression of cognitive decline.

MIND Diet

  • Leafy green vegetables daily
  • Nuts as healthy snacks
  • Fish rich in omega-3 (weekly)
  • Whole grains & olive oil

Quality Sleep

  • Aim for 7-8 hours nightly
  • Maintain consistent sleep schedule
  • Treat sleep disorders promptly
  • Avoid screens before bed

Risk Reduction

  • Manage blood pressure/diabetes
  • Avoid smoking & excess alcohol
  • Protect head from injuries
  • Regular health check-ups

Prevention Tips Overview

Adopt these simple lifestyle habits proven to support brain health:

  • Stay physically active (aerobic exercise)
  • Engage in mental stimulation (puzzles, learning)
  • Maintain social connections
  • Follow a brain-healthy diet (like the MIND diet)
  • Manage cardiovascular health (BP, diabetes, cholesterol)
  • Get quality, consistent sleep
  • Protect your head from injury
  • Limit alcohol consumption and avoid smoking

Need Professional Help?

If you're experiencing memory problems or have concerns, consult with a healthcare professional immediately. Early diagnosis leads to better intervention options.

Frequently Asked Questions

What's the difference between Alzheimer's and dementia?

Dementia is an umbrella term for symptoms affecting memory and thinking. Alzheimer's is the most common cause of dementia, accounting for 60-80% of cases.

At what age does Alzheimer's typically start?

Most people with Alzheimer's are 65 or older. However, early-onset Alzheimer's can occur in people in their 40s or 50s, though it's less common.

Is Alzheimer's hereditary?

Having a parent or sibling with Alzheimer's increases risk, but it doesn't mean you'll develop it. Lifestyle factors also play a significant role.

Can Alzheimer's be prevented?

While there's no guaranteed prevention, research suggests healthy lifestyle choices—exercise, diet, mental stimulation, and social engagement—may reduce risk.
